Introduction

LEGO-MR is a concept project exploring how mixed reality can enhance the fun and collaboration of building LEGO sets. In this scenario, a parent and their teenager use a mobile app and a pair of MR glasses to build and interact with LEGO sets in new ways. The phone helps with set selection, instructions, and pairing, while the MR headset overlays virtual guidance and step-by-step simulations on top of the real world. The project was built as a visual prototype in Figma for our Human-Computer Interaction course, with a focus on user-friendly interaction, creativity, and accessibility.

Interaction Design Patterns

1.1. Mobile App

  • Navigation: The app opens directly on the dashboard, which is accessible through a bottom navigation bar. This ensures quick access to essential sections like Home, Sets, and Profile.
  • Layout: Horizontal carousels display LEGO sets using large images and tiles for visual engagement. The layout also includes clear information panels for each set (e.g., name, overview, rating).
  • Actions and Commands: Users interact with tap gestures to select sets and navigate between pages. Standard mobile actions like swipe and tap are used throughout the app.
  • Tables and Lists: Content such as ratings and set details are displayed in list-like vertical or horizontal arrangements using cards for clarity and accessibility.
  • Form Patterns: Any input screens (e.g., login or feedback) follow standard mobile form UI with large touch-friendly inputs and on-screen keyboards.

1.2. MR Headset

  • Virtual/3D Objects: During Build mode, digital overlays highlight where pieces go in the physical LEGO model.
  • AR/Spatial Markers: The glasses track the physical LEGO blocks and guide placement with AR highlights.
  • Buttons & Menus: Simple, floating UI elements allow users to access more information or quit the session during MR use.
  • Gestures: Basic selection and navigation is imagined through head gaze or hand-based triggers (depending on device capabilities), although interaction is mostly visual.
  • Text & Labels: Clear labels are shown for each brick and build step, improving visibility and understanding.
  • Audio/Sound Effects: Future improvements may include audio guidance or confirmation sounds to enhance the immersive experience.
  • Camera Controls: The MR headset passively captures and overlays content in the user's environment, enhancing the build with AR.

These patterns were chosen to make the LEGO experience feel natural, intuitive, and magical—preserving the hands-on fun of LEGO while enhancing it through technology.

Design Decisions

The design of LEGO-MR was heavily inspired by the playful, creative spirit of LEGO itself. We wanted to make sure the app felt fun and intuitive while being functional for both kids and adults.

2.1. Color Scheme

We went with bright, saturated colors that reflect the joy and energy of LEGO bricks. The primary red (#F93827), sunny yellow (#FFD65A), and vibrant green (#16C47F) create a playful vibe. These colors are contrasted with a soft gray background (#ededed) and clean black text (#180d0c) for readability.

2.2. Typography

All fonts were chosen from Google Fonts to keep things accessible and cohesive. Titles use the bold and chunky Sigmar One, subtitles use Sigmar, and the body text is written in Yusei Magic for a touch of whimsy without sacrificing clarity.

2.3. Iconography & Visual Style

Icons are consistent in style and placed intuitively across the interface, ensuring quick recognition and minimal learning curve. The UI is slightly rounded and minimal, letting the vibrant content (like LEGO models and AR overlays) take center stage.

2.4. Platform Transition

The experience transitions smoothly between mobile and MR platforms. The phone is used for browsing, selection, and setup, while the MR glasses handle immersive interaction during building. We made sure the switch feels natural—users get onboarding instructions when pairing the headset, and MR mode reflects the mobile layout for familiarity.

2.5. Accessibility & Usability

We prioritized simplicity and visibility. Buttons are large enough for young users, colors are high contrast for clarity, and instructions are icon-supported where possible. The design encourages exploration while keeping users oriented at all times.

User Manual

3.1. Getting Started

  1. Download and install the LEGO-MR mobile app from your app store.
  2. Launch the app and optionally pair it with your MR headset using Bluetooth or Wi-Fi.

3.2. Browsing LEGO Sets

Once inside the app, users can browse:

  • Owned LEGO sets
  • Popular and trending sets
  • Newly released sets
  • etc.

Clicking on any set opens its details: name, overview, gallery, build time, rating, etc. From here, the user can press:

  • Build - to begin step-by-step assembly
  • Play - additional features under consideration

Aside from searching for sets and seeing their information, the user can add sets to their cart for future purchases or to manage their account.

3.3. Entering Build Mode

  1. After pressing "Build", the user can choose to connect AR glasses or continue without them.
  2. If glasses are not connected, a step-by-step instruction page opens directly on the phone.
  3. If glasses are connected, the user is guided through connection and calibration before entering MR Build mode.

3.4. MR Build Mode

With the headset connected, the user sees:

  • Set info: Name, category, rating
  • Controls: Quit button, Overview button (opens set description)
  • Status Panel: Remaining steps, time spent, estimated time left
  • Piece Placement: Each LEGO piece is labeled, and its placement region is visually highlighted
  • Expanded Steps Panel: Displays the full instruction step for reference (same as mobile version of the Mode)

3.5. Completion

When all pieces are placed, the status panel is replaced with the LEGO logo and a "FINISHED" label in the bottom-left corner of the display.

Conclusion

LEGO-MR was a fun and creative concept to work on, combining everyday technology like smartphones with the immersive power of MR glasses. It gave us the chance to think about real-world usability, accessibility, and how digital tools can enhance physical experiences. We focused on a simple and intuitive user flow, keeping both kids and parents in mind. While it's not a real app (yet!), the Figma prototype shows what could be possible with just a little imagination and the right tech.
